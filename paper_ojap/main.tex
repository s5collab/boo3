\begin{abstract}
We present updated systemic properties of the ultra-faint dwarf galaxy Boötes~III using spectroscopic data from the Southern Stellar Stream Spectroscopic Survey (S$^5$) DR2 and astrometry from \gaia DR3.
From a Gaussian-mixture-model fit to S$^5$ DR2, we identify 21 high-probability members and measure a velocity dispersion of $\sigma_{v} = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$ km s$^{-1}$---more than five times smaller than the previously reported $10.7 \pm 3.5$ km s$^{-1}$---and a mean metallicity of [Fe/H] $= -2.34 \pm 0.11$. The revised dispersion brings Boötes~III in line with other tidally disrupting dwarfs such as Antlia~2 and Crater~2.
Orbit integrations in a combined Milky Way (MW) + Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) potential confirm a highly eccentric ($e \approx 0.82$), retrograde, polar orbit with a recent pericentric passage $\sim 0.13$~Gyr ago at $r_{\mathrm{peri}} \approx 9$~kpc. The new low dispersion implies a low half-light dynamical mass and an internal density below the enclosed Milky Way density at orbital pericenter; equivalently, the King tidal radius at pericenter is $r_t \approx 164$~pc ($\sim 0.3\,r_h$), much smaller than the half-light radius---strongly supporting active tidal disruption.
We simulate tidal streams and show that the predicted track and kinematics are sensitive to the MW halo mass, LMC mass, and Solar velocity; the simulated streams are broadly consistent with the Styx stream.
In action / integrals-of-motion space, Boötes~III overlaps the Typhon stream, suggesting a common infall origin (though distinct metallicities rule out a direct progenitor--debris relationship).
Our tidal-tail search yielded no detection because the Sagittarius stream contaminates the same sky-position and proper-motion footprint; deep spectroscopic follow-up is essential to confirm the Boötes~III--Styx connection.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} \label{section:intro}

Dwarf spheroidal galaxies (dSphs) are among the most dark matter-dominated systems known, making them powerful probes of the nature of dark matter \citep[see, e.g.,][]{Bullock2017ARAA}.
Their satellite abundance can constrain warm and fuzzy dark matter particle masses \citep{Nadler2021}, while their internal kinematics can constrain the self-interaction cross section of self-interacting dark matter.
However, inferring the dark matter density profile from kinematics alone is challenging due to the degeneracy between the density profile and the velocity anisotropy \citep{Lokas2002, Guerra2023}.

The tidal disruption of dSphs offers an alternative approach to constraining their dark matter density profiles.
The dense, cuspy profiles predicted by cold dark matter (CDM) are far more resilient to tidal stripping than the cored profiles arising in self-interacting dark matter models \citep{Penarrubia2010}. \citet{Errani2023} demonstrated that cuspy Navarro--Frenk--White (NFW) subhaloes never fully disrupt, whereas cored subhaloes undergo runaway mass loss once tides penetrate the core, leading to complete destruction. The survival or disruption of a dwarf galaxy on a close orbit therefore places direct constraints on the maximum core size permitted by observations, and thus provides insights into the nature of dark matter.

Wide-field photometric surveys---including the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS), the Dark Energy Survey (DES), and the Panoramic Survey Telescope and Rapid Response System (Pan-STARRS)---have driven the rapid discovery of ultra-faint dwarf galaxies and stellar streams over the past two decades \citep[see][and references therein]{Simon2019, Koposov2015, Martin2015, Luque2017, Koposov2018, Torrealba2018, Homma2019, DrlicaWagner2020, Mau2020, Cerny2022, Cerny2023}. Interpreting the tidal features of these systems requires careful modeling of the gravitational potential, including the influence of the Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC). The LMC induces a barycentric acceleration of the Milky Way (MW) that significantly perturbs satellite orbits and the morphology of tidal streams \citep{Erkal2019, Erkal2020, Nadler2020}. \citet{Pace2022} computed orbits for 46~dSphs in a combined MW+LMC potential and found that 40\% of their sample experienced a $>25\%$ change in pericenter and/or apocenter when the LMC was included. They further identified several systems likely undergoing tidal disruption based on comparing each satellite's average density to the MW density at its orbital pericenter; we apply the same diagnostic to Boo~III in Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}, and several of these tidally disrupting candidates have been targets of detailed spectroscopic follow-up described below.

Spectroscopic characterization of these faint systems has been greatly advanced by the Southern Stellar Stream Spectroscopic Survey (\SSSSS; \citealt{Li2019, Li2022}), which uses the AAOmega spectrograph \citep{Sharp2006} on the 3.9\,m Anglo-Australian Telescope (AAT). Originally designed to map stellar streams in the Southern Hemisphere, \SSSSS has also enabled detailed spectroscopic studies of dwarf galaxies and their tidal features.

Besides the well-known Sagittarius dwarf and its extensive tidal stream \citep{Vasiliev2021}, several other MW satellites have been identified as likely undergoing tidal disruption, including Tucana~III \citep{DrlicaWagner2015, Simon2017}, Antlia~2 \citep{Torrealba2019}, and Crater~2 \citep{Torrealba2016, Caldwell2017}. Spectroscopic follow-up with the AAT has provided key evidence in each case. In a pathfinder program of \SSSSS\ that demonstrated the feasibility of spectroscopic stream studies with AAT/AAOmega and motivated the design of \SSSSS, \citet{Li2018tucIII} identified members in the tidal tails of Tucana~III and measured a velocity gradient along the stream, establishing it as likely the first tidally disrupted ultra-faint dwarf galaxy. Subsequently within \SSSSS, \citet{Ji2021} detected a velocity gradient aligned with the orbital direction in Antlia~2 and a tentative gradient in Crater~2; \citet{Limberg2025} then spectroscopically confirmed Crater~2's stellar stream and used the ratio of stream-to-galaxy velocity dispersion to constrain its dark matter density profile. In a related study, \citet{Sandford2025} reported a tentative velocity gradient in Bo\"{o}tes~I aligned with its orbital direction, which, if confirmed, could indicate incipient tidal disruption in that system as well.

\boo (hereafter Boo~III) is an ultra-faint dwarf galaxy discovered by \citet{Grillmair2009} as a stellar overdensity in SDSS photometry. With a semi-major half-light radius of $a_h \sim 0.55$\,kpc (corresponding to an azimuthally-averaged $r_h \sim 0.45$\,kpc) \citep{Moskowitz2020}, Boo~III is unusually extended for its luminosity ($M_V \sim -5.8$), giving it a low surface brightness that led to early speculation that the system is being tidally stripped \citep{Carlin2009, Correnti2009}. \citet{Carlin2009} conducted the first spectroscopic study of Boo~III, measuring a systemic velocity of $v_{\mathrm{hel}} = 197.5 \pm 3.8$ \vlosUnits and a velocity dispersion of $\sigma_v = 14.0^{+3.2}_{-3.1}$ \vlosUnits from 20 probable members observed with the Multi-Mirror Telescope (MMT)/Hectospec. \citet{Carlin2018} subsequently refined the velocity dispersion to $\sigma_v = 10.7 \pm 3.5$ \vlosUnits through an improved membership analysis and measured proper motions from \gaia DR2, revealing a retrograde, highly eccentric orbit. Crucially, the proper motions matched those of the Styx stellar stream \citep{Grillmair2009}, suggesting a physical association that would make Boo~III a rare example of an ultra-faint dwarf galaxy caught in the act of tidal disruption.

The large velocity dispersion reported in these earlier studies is, however, puzzling when compared to other tidally disrupting dwarfs. Both Antlia~2 and Crater~2 have much smaller velocity dispersions---$\sigma_v = 5.98$ \vlosUnits \citep{Ji2021} and $\sigma_v = 2.51$ \vlosUnits \citep{Limberg2025}, respectively---despite having significantly larger half-light radii ($\sim 2.9$\,kpc and $\sim 1.1$\,kpc). This raises the question of whether the previously measured velocity dispersion of Boo~III was inflated by contamination or small sample size, or whether Boo~III has a fundamentally different tidal evolution history.

In this work, we address this question using new spectroscopic data from \SSSSS DR2 combined with astrometry from \gaia DR3. We re-measure Boo~III's systemic velocity, velocity dispersion, proper motion, and metallicity, and use these updated parameters to integrate its orbit in a MW+LMC potential and generate mock tidal streams to assess the Boo~III--Styx connection. This paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{section:data} introduces the \SSSSS\ DR2 spectroscopic data and the cleaning cuts applied to build the input catalog of candidate Boo~III stars. Section~\ref{section:membership} presents the Gaussian-mixture-model (GMM) membership analysis that simultaneously re-derives Boo~III's systemic heliocentric velocity, line-of-sight velocity dispersion, mean metallicity, intrinsic metallicity dispersion, and \gaia\ DR3 proper motion, together with an updated spatial centroid, and compares them with previous spectroscopic studies. Section~\ref{section:distance} derives a new heliocentric distance from five \gaia\ DR3 RR~Lyrae stars in the field. In Section~\ref{section:orbit} we integrate Boo~III's orbit and simulate tidal streams using \texttt{galpy} \citep{Bovy2015}, comparing results with and without the LMC. Section~\ref{section:discussion} discusses the implications: evidence for active tidal disruption from the orbit, density and tidal-radius arguments; velocity-gradient measurements and comparison with predictions from $N$-body and particle spray simulations; the association with the Typhon stream in integrals-of-motion and action space; and the observational search for an extended Boo~III tidal tail. We conclude in Section~\ref{section:conclusion}.


\section{Data} \label{section:data}

\subsection{\SSSSS\ Spectroscopy}
This paper uses data from the second public data release (DR2) of the Southern Stellar Stream Spectroscopic Survey (\SSSSS; T.\,S.~Li et al., in preparation).
\SSSSS\ is a spectroscopic follow-up of stellar streams and dwarf galaxies---particularly those potentially undergoing tidal stripping---discovered in photometric surveys in the southern hemisphere, carried out with the dual-arm AAOmega spectrograph \citep{Sharp2006} fed by the Two-degree Field (2dF; \citealt{Lewis2002}) fiber positioner on the 3.9\,m Anglo-Australian Telescope (AAT).
Each AAT configuration observes up to 392 science fibers simultaneously over a 2\ensuremath{^{\circ}}-diameter field of view, yielding a blue-arm low-resolution spectrum ($3700$--$5800$\,\AA, $R \sim 1300$) used for stellar parameters and metallicity, and a red-arm high-resolution spectrum ($8420$--$8820$\,\AA, $R \sim 10{,}000$) covering the calcium triplet (CaT) region for precise radial velocities.
The survey design, target selection strategy, and data reduction pipeline are described in detail in \citet{Li2019} and \citet{Li2022}.
Boo~III was observed over multiple AAT nights and covered with several 2dF pointings centred on \citet{Grillmair2009}'s reported centroid, with targets drawn from \gaia\ DR3 \citep{GaiaMission, GaiaDR3} astrometry and DECam photometry from the DECaLS DR8 catalogue \citep{DECaLS}, following the standard \SSSSS\ targeting strategy: candidate members were required to be consistent with the colour--magnitude diagram (CMD) of an old, metal-poor stellar population at the distance of Boo~III and to have proper motions broadly consistent with the system. Throughout this work the photometry quoted as $g_0, r_0$ refers to the dereddened magnitudes with the \citet{Schlegel1998} $E(B-V)$ map and the DES DR1 DECam extinction coefficients ($R_g = 3.185$, $R_r = 2.140$, $R_i = 1.569$, $R_z = 1.196$; \citealt{Abbott2018_DESDR1}); the same dereddening is used for both target selection and the membership / CMD analysis below.

Stellar parameters ($T_{\rm eff}$, $\log g$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$) and heliocentric radial velocities are derived by jointly fitting the blue- and red-arm spectra against the \texttt{rvspecfit} template grid \citep{Koposov2019_rvspecfit} using the \SSSSS\ spectral-fitting pipeline. DR2 includes a number of improvements to this pipeline relative to DR1, summarised in \citet{Ji2021}, \citet{Sandford2025}, \citet{Limberg2025}, and T.\,S.~Li et al., in preparation, that yield more robust RV and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ measurements, particularly for faint low signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR) stars.
We adopt the DR2 catalog throughout this paper.

\subsection{Input Sample Selection} \label{section:input_sample} 
Sky positions are measured as angular separations from the Boo~III centroid $(\alpha_0,\delta_0)$, taken initially from \citet{Grillmair2009} for the target selection.  This centroid is re-derived self-consistently in Section~\ref{section:membership} from the 21 high-probability GMM members and the updated value, $(\alpha_0,\delta_0) = (209.557^\circ, 26.553^\circ)$, is used in all subsequent analysis.  The tangent-plane coordinates are computed as
\begin{align}
\xi  &= \cos\delta\,\sin(\alpha-\alpha_0), \\
\eta &= \sin\delta\cos\delta_0 - \cos\delta\sin\delta_0\cos(\alpha-\alpha_0),
\end{align}
i.e.\ the orthographic (SIN) projection of the celestial sphere onto the plane tangent at $(\alpha_0,\delta_0)$, defining the tangent-plane coordinates $(\xi, \eta)$ used throughout this paper.  In the small-angle limit relevant here, $\xi \approx \Delta\alpha\cos\delta$ and $\eta \approx \Delta\delta$.
We then apply the following quality and sanity cuts to all \SSSSS\ DR2 sources that were targeted as part of the Boo~III field:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=0pt]
    \item signal-to-noise ratio \texttt{best\_sn\_1700d} $> 2$;
    \item heliocentric RV uncertainty \texttt{vel\_calib\_std} $< 10$~\vlosUnits;
    \item good-star probability flag \texttt{good\_star\_pb} $> 0.5$;
    \item finite \gaia\ DR3 proper motions in both components;
    \item $|v_{\rm hel}| < 600$~\vlosUnits\ (to exclude pipeline failures);
    \item $[\mathrm{Fe/H}] < 0$~dex (excluding metal-rich disk stars).
\end{itemize}
All structural parameters of Boo~III used in this work are taken from the literature; the adopted values, including the ellipticity, position angle, and the circularised half-light radius $r_h$ (related to the semi-major-axis half-light radius by $a_h = r_h / \sqrt{1-\epsilon}$), are listed in Table~\ref{tab:params}. Throughout this paper we use $r_h = 33.03'$ \citep{Moskowitz2020} as the canonical half-light radius unless otherwise noted.

We restrict the spatial footprint to within the 3 $r_h$ ellipse, i.e.\ an elliptical aperture with semi-major axis $3\,a_h$. An elliptical aperture, rather than a simple circle, is chosen to best reflect the observed light distribution of the system.
We also apply a $4\times 4$~\pmUnits\ proper-motion (PM) box centred on the \citet{Pace2022} Boo~III value $(\mu_\alpha\cos\delta, \mu_\delta) \simeq (-1.17, -0.89)$~\pmUnits, i.e.\ $(\mu_\alpha\cos\delta, \mu_\delta) \in [-3.17, +0.83] \times [-2.88, +1.12]$~\pmUnits, to restrict the input catalog to the region of PM space where Boo~III members can plausibly lie.  This box is also consistent with the earlier \gaia~DR2 measurement of \citet{Carlin2018}, $(-1.14, -0.98) \pm (0.18, 0.20)$~\pmUnits.
Finally, we cross-match the remaining stars to the \gaia\ DR3 RR~Lyrae catalogue \citep{GaiaRRL_reference} within a 2$''$ tolerance and remove all RR~Lyrae cross-matches from the input catalog; their pulsation-induced RV scatter would otherwise bias the kinematic fit of Section~\ref{section:membership}. We do not impose an explicit blue-horizontal-branch (BHB) filter, but the per-star RV-uncertainty cut (\texttt{vel\_calib\_std} $< 10$~\vlosUnits) implicitly removes most BHB candidates at Boo~III's $g \approx 18.4$ horizontal branch, since their broader hydrogen-line absorption produces per-star RV errors typically $\gtrsim 15$~\vlosUnits\ in \SSSSS\ at that magnitude. The downstream effect of this implicit BHB exclusion is discussed further in the \citet{Geha2026} comparison of Section~\ref{section:lit_compare}.
After all of the above cuts, the input catalog consists of 127 non-RRL stars within the 3 $r_h$ ellipse centred on the \citet{Grillmair2009} discovery position; the same cuts evaluated about the updated centroid derived in Section~\ref{section:membership} below (which adopts $(\alpha_0,\delta_0)_{\rm new} = (209.557^\circ, 26.553^\circ)$) yield 120 stars.  We have re-run the GMM membership analysis on both the 127-star (original-centre) and 120-star (updated-centre) input catalogues and recover the same 21 high-probability ($P_{\rm mem} > 0.95$) members in both cases; results quoted in the remainder of the paper are from the 120-star, updated-centre fit. This catalog is the input for the Gaussian-mixture-model membership classification described in Section~\ref{section:membership}.

\section{Membership and Systemic Properties} \label{section:membership}

Rather than identifying Boo~III members by successive hand-tuned RV and CMD box cuts, we classify the input catalog from Section~\ref{section:input_sample} with a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) that simultaneously fits Boo~III's systemic properties and the Milky Way (MW) field-star background. The model and sampling scheme follow the mixture-model framework of \citet{Pace2019} and \citet{Pace2022} for the astrometric and spatial components, and \citet{Sandford2025} for the joint inclusion of $v_{\rm hel}$ and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ in the likelihood, on which the present implementation is most closely modelled. We refer the reader to those works for the full likelihood derivation.

\subsection{Mixture Model}
Each star $i$ is assigned a likelihood that is the weighted sum of a dwarf-galaxy (``gal'') component and an MW field-background (``bg'') component:
% or add a cross-reference.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:gmm_mix}
\mathcal{L}_i = f_{\rm mem}\,\mathcal{L}_i^{\rm gal} + (1 - f_{\rm mem})\,\mathcal{L}_i^{\rm bg},
\end{equation}
where $f_{\rm mem}$ is the galaxy membership fraction, a free parameter of the fit.

For each observable ($v_{\rm hel}$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, $\mu_\alpha\cos\delta$, $\mu_\delta$), the \emph{galaxy} component is a Gaussian centred at the systemic value with a scatter that combines the intrinsic dispersion and the per-star measurement error in quadrature. For $v_{\rm hel}$ and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ we fit both the mean and a constant intrinsic dispersion (four parameters: $\bar v_{\rm hel}$, $\sigma_v$, $\overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}$, $\sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}$); for the proper motions we fit only the two means ($\bar\mu_\alpha\cos\delta$, $\bar\mu_\delta$), with the intrinsic PM dispersion fixed to zero because the small ($\sim 1$~\vlosUnits) line-of-sight velocity dispersion of Boo~III is unresolved by \gaia\ at the heliocentric distance of Boo~III ($d = 48.5$~kpc, derived in Section~\ref{section:distance}).

The \emph{MW field background} is modelled as a truncated Gaussian in $v_{\rm hel}$ with mean $\bar v^{\rm bg}$ and dispersion $\sigma_v^{\rm bg}$ (truncated to $|v_{\rm hel}| < 600$~\vlosUnits), times a truncated Gaussian in $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ with mean $\overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}^{\rm bg}$ and dispersion $\sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}^{\rm bg}$ (truncated to $[\mathrm{Fe/H}] \in [-4, 0]$, the range of the \texttt{rvspecfit} template grid), times a uniform density over the $4 \times 4$~\pmUnits\ PM box of Section~\ref{section:input_sample}. The truncated-Gaussian forms in $v_{\rm hel}$ and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ capture the actual peaked, non-uniform distribution of MW field stars in the Boo~III phase-space neighbourhood; the uniform PM background is appropriate because the PM box was already tuned to bracket Boo~III, so the residual MW contamination is approximately flat in $(\mu_\alpha\cos\delta, \mu_\delta)$.

The GMM therefore samples a total of eleven parameters: the membership fraction $f_{\rm mem}$; the six systemic galaxy parameters ($\bar v_{\rm hel}, \sigma_v, \overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}, \sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}, \bar\mu_\alpha\cos\delta, \bar\mu_\delta$); and the four MW field-background parameters ($\bar v^{\rm bg}, \sigma_v^{\rm bg}, \overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}^{\rm bg}, \sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}^{\rm bg}$). We adopt deliberately weakly informative priors that do not depend on any published Boo~III systemic value: linear-uniform priors on all means (with the four galaxy parameter ranges set to the orange box of Figure~\ref{fig:main_members}, i.e.\ Eqs.~\ref{eq:boxcut_rv}--\ref{eq:boxcut_feh}; the background mean ranges are set to the full input box of Section~\ref{section:input_sample}) and log-uniform (Jeffreys') priors on all dispersions. The full prior table is given in Table~\ref{tab:priors}.

\subsection{Fit Results}
We sample the posterior with \texttt{emcee} \citep{emcee}, using 64 walkers run for 8000 steps with a 2000-step burn-in. The mean acceptance fraction across walkers is $\approx 0.39$ --- the fraction of proposed steps that are accepted by the Metropolis criterion, averaged over the chain --- comfortably within the $0.2$--$0.5$ range typically considered well-mixed for an affine-invariant ensemble sampler. The chain is flattened and thinned by a factor of 10, yielding $\sim 38{,}000$ post-burn-in posterior samples.
We find a systemic heliocentric velocity of $\bar v_{\rm hel} = 191.22^{+0.65}_{-0.80}$~\vlosUnits\ and a small velocity dispersion of $\sigma_v = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$~\vlosUnits.
The systemic proper motion is $(\bar\mu_\alpha\cos\delta, \bar\mu_\delta) = (-1.163^{+0.017}_{-0.019}, -0.883^{+0.013}_{-0.014})$~\pmUnits, and the mean metallicity and intrinsic metallicity dispersion are $\overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]} = -2.34 \pm 0.11$~dex and $\sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]} = 0.36^{+0.10}_{-0.08}$~dex.
Our velocity dispersion is more than five times smaller than the $10.7 \pm 3.5$~\vlosUnits\ reported by \citet{Carlin2018}, and our systemic velocity is offset from theirs by $\sim 6$~\vlosUnits; the metallicity is consistent with \citet{Carlin2018} within the joint uncertainties.  Our systemic proper motion is consistent with both the \gaia~DR2 measurement of \citet{Carlin2018} ($(-1.14, -0.98) \pm (0.18, 0.20)$~\pmUnits, offset of $0.02$ and $0.10$~\pmUnits, well inside their $\sim 0.2$~\pmUnits\ errors) and the more precise \gaia~DR3 measurement of \citet{Pace2022} ($(-1.168, -0.890) \pm (0.018, 0.014)$~\pmUnits, offset of $0.005$ and $0.007$~\pmUnits, well inside their $\lesssim 0.02$~\pmUnits\ errors); the agreement with Pace+22 is tighter because both works use \gaia\ DR3. A detailed comparison with prior spectroscopic measurements---\citet{Carlin2009}, \citet{Carlin2018}, and the recent \citet{Geha2026} DEIMOS sample---is presented in Section~\ref{section:lit_compare}.
The posterior corner plot of the six Boo~III galaxy parameters plus the membership fraction $f_{\rm mem}$ is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:boo3_params} (the four MW field-background nuisance parameters are also fit but omitted for clarity), and all systemic parameters are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:params}.

\subsection{Membership Assignments}
The GMM yields an exceptionally clean membership classification.
Of the 120 input stars, 21 have $P_{\rm mem} > 0.95$ (20 with $P_{\rm mem} > 0.99$), while 99 have $P_{\rm mem} < 0.01$.
There are \emph{no} stars in the ambiguous $0.01 < P_{\rm mem} < 0.95$ regime.
This bimodal membership distribution reflects the fact that Boo~III is kinematically and chemically well separated from the MW field in all four observables.
We verify this classification by applying a hand-tuned box cut to the same input catalog.
The box cut selects stars satisfying all four of the conditions below (illustrated as orange dashed rectangles in the proper-motion and RV-vs-$[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ panels of Figure~\ref{fig:main_members}):
\begin{align}
170 &< v_{\rm hel} < 210~\mathrm{km~s^{-1}}, \label{eq:boxcut_rv}\\
-2.0 &< \mu_\alpha\cos\delta < -0.5~\mathrm{mas\,yr^{-1}}, \label{eq:boxcut_pmra}\\
-1.5 &< \mu_\delta <  0.0~\mathrm{mas\,yr^{-1}}, \label{eq:boxcut_pmdec}\\
[\mathrm{Fe/H}] &< -1.2. \label{eq:boxcut_feh}
\end{align}
The box cut recovers identically the same 21 stars as the GMM, confirming that the membership assignment is robust to the choice of method. Because this simple cut is a faithful surrogate for the full mixture-model classification, we adopt it as the search criterion for tidal-tail candidates beyond the 3 $r_h$ ellipse in Appendix~\ref{section:appendix_tail}.

\subsubsection{Updated centroid}
With the 21 GMM members in hand we re-derive the Boo~III centre by iterating the median (RA, Dec) of all members within $1\,r_h$ of the running centre, using the 1\,$r_h$ ellipse (semi-major $a_h = 40.4'$, semi-minor $b_h = 27.0'$, oriented along PA = $278.91^\circ$ E of N; same shape as the photometric isophote of \citealt{Moskowitz2020}). Starting from the \citet{Grillmair2009} discovery centre, the iteration converges in three steps to $(\alpha, \delta)_{\rm new} = (209.557^\circ \pm 0.3^\circ,\ 26.553^\circ \pm 0.3^\circ)$, with 11 members inside $1\,r_h$ at convergence. The new centre is offset by $\xi = +13.8'$ (east) and $\eta = -14.8'$ (south) --- a total shift of $20.2'$, or $0.61\,r_h$. The shift reflects an asymmetry in the spatial distribution of the spectroscopically-confirmed members within the $3\,r_h$ ellipse: a clump of high-probability members sits $\sim 20'$ south-east of the \citet{Grillmair2009} centre (Figure~\ref{fig:main_members}, left panel). The $\pm 0.3^\circ$ uncertainty is a deliberately conservative choice that brackets the full shift from the literature \citet{Grillmair2009} centroid to our updated value; this $\pm 0.3^\circ$ width then propagates into the Monte-Carlo (MC) orbit integrations of Section~\ref{section:orbit}. Because the \SSSSS\ target selection function is approximately uniform over the field of view, the offset is unlikely to be an artefact of selection bias and instead reflects a genuine shift of the Boo~III centroid as traced by its spectroscopically-confirmed members.  The earlier \citet{Carlin2018} and \citet{Geha2026} spectroscopic samples used a single $\sim 1\,r_h$ pointing centred near the \citet{Grillmair2009} discovery position and therefore have very limited leverage on the larger-scale centroid; their member sub-samples cluster tightly within $\lesssim 1\,r_h$ of that pointing by construction.  Boo~III's only published structural fit \citep{Moskowitz2020} adopts the \citet{Grillmair2009} centroid (itself a by-eye estimate from the SDSS discovery image) as a fixed prior rather than fitting it.  Given these constraints, our updated centroid is best understood as a refined spatial centre, anchored to the on-sky positions of the spectroscopically-confirmed members across the full $3\,r_h$ extent, rather than as a revision of the published photometric profile.
\subsubsection{RR Lyrae members}
We also examine the RR~Lyrae within the 3 $r_h$ ellipse from \gaia\ DR3 (i.e. those removed from the input catalog in Section~\ref{section:input_sample}).
Three of them fall within the hand-tuned box-cut region (Eqs.~\ref{eq:boxcut_rv}--\ref{eq:boxcut_pmdec}) and have apparent magnitudes $g_0 \in [18.5, 19.5]$, consistent with an RR~Lyrae at the approximate distance of Boo~III. \subsection{Comparison with Previous Spectroscopic Studies} \label{section:lit_compare}
We compare our \SSSSS\ DR2 + \gaia\ DR3 measurements with the three published spectroscopic studies of Boo~III: the discovery-era MMT/Hectospec analyses of \citet{Carlin2009} and \citet{Carlin2018}, and the more recent Keck/DEIMOS catalog of \citet{Geha2026}.

\subsubsection{Comparison with \citet{Carlin2009, Carlin2018}}
\citet{Carlin2009} observed Boo~III with MMT/Hectospec---a 300-fibre, 1$^\circ$-diameter field of view---using a single pointing centred on the Boo~III centroid, and identified 20 probable members yielding $\bar v_{\rm hel} = 197.5 \pm 3.3$~\vlosUnits\ and $\sigma_v = 14.0^{+3.2}_{-3.1}$~\vlosUnits.  \citet{Carlin2018} reanalysed the same data with refined membership criteria and additional Gaia DR2 proper motions, lowering the dispersion to $10.7 \pm 3.5$~\vlosUnits\ for 16 retained members.  Their 16-member sample includes one \gaia\ DR3 RR Lyrae star (their \texttt{BooIII\_RR1}; the same star now used in our 5-RRL distance derivation, Section~\ref{section:distance}); the remaining 15 are primarily RGB stars.  None of the 16 Carlin members have a counterpart within 2$''$ of any of our 21 GMM members: their sample is systematically fainter ($g_{\rm PanSTARRS}$ median 20.1, faintest 21.0, versus our median $g_0 = 18.5$, faintest 19.8), so most stars are below our effective S/N limit and the two datasets sample largely disjoint Boo~III populations.  Our measurements differ from \citet{Carlin2018} in two ways: $\sigma_v$ is more than five times smaller, and $\bar v_{\rm hel}$ is offset by $\sim 6$~\vlosUnits.  The discrepancy is consistent with the different sample brightness and the higher per-star RV precision of \SSSSS\ ($\sigma_{v_{\rm hel}} \lesssim 3$~\vlosUnits\ at $g \lesssim 19$, versus median $\sigma_{v_{\rm hel}} \approx 10$~\vlosUnits\ for the Hectospec data).  Our $\sigma_v$ is also based solely on the 21 RGB members (RR~Lyrae and HB-type stars are excluded from the GMM input because pulsation- and broader-line effects can inflate per-star RV scatter), whereas the Carlin sample contains one RRL whose single-epoch Hectospec RV (173~\vlosUnits) sits at the low-velocity tail of their distribution and contributes to inflating $\sigma_v$.

\subsubsection{Comparison with \citet{Geha2026}}
The recent Keck/DEIMOS catalog of \citet{Geha2026} reports a Boo~III dispersion of $\sigma_v = 5.27^{+2.10}_{-1.67}$~\vlosUnits\ from 16 member stars (their $P_{\rm mem,novar} > 0.5$ sample --- i.e.\ $P_{\rm mem} > 0.5$ \emph{and} not flagged as a velocity variable), also using a smaller, single-mask field of view than \SSSSS.  Of these 16, 5 carry their horizontal-branch flag (likely BHB stars; see below) and 11 do not (likely RGB).  The Gaia DR3 RR~Lyrae from Geha+26's broader catalog that we adopt in our 5-RRL distance derivation (Section~\ref{section:distance}) is \emph{not} in their 16-member $\sigma_v$ sample --- it is present in their broader $P_{\rm mem} > 0.5$ catalog but excluded by their velocity-variable cut.  The 5 horizontal-branch-flagged stars are essentially all blue horizontal-branch (BHB) stars at Boo~III's low metallicity, since the red-HB locus is depopulated at $[\mathrm{Fe/H}] \lesssim -2$.  In \SSSSS\ data the per-star RV uncertainty for BHB stars at Boo~III's $g \approx 18.4$ horizontal branch is typically $\gtrsim 15$~\vlosUnits\ --- larger than for RGB stars at the same magnitude --- because the broader hydrogen-line features and the lower S/N at the \SSSSS\ magnitude limit combine to wash out the narrow spectral features used by \texttt{rvspecfit}; the $\sigma_{v_{\rm hel}} < 10$~\vlosUnits\ cut of Section~\ref{section:input_sample} therefore implicitly excludes them, leaving our 21-member sample effectively purely RGB after the explicit cross-match removal of \gaia\ DR3 RR~Lyrae.  Geha+26's Keck/DEIMOS spectra, by contrast, retain BHB members at small per-star RV uncertainty (median $\sigma_{v_{\rm hel}} \approx 3$~\vlosUnits\ for their 5 HB-flagged stars), so the inclusion vs.\ exclusion of BHB stars is a real sample-composition difference between the two surveys --- not a difference in per-star error budget within either survey.  Their systemic velocity, $\bar v_{\rm hel} = 189.9 \pm 1.8$~\vlosUnits, agrees with our $191.22^{+0.65}_{-0.80}$~\vlosUnits\ at $\sim 1.4\sigma$.  A direct on-sky cross-match between our 21 GMM members and Geha+26's catalog finds that none of our 21 lies within $2''$ of any of Geha+26's 16-star $\sigma_v$ sample (the $P_{\rm mem,novar} > 0.5$ sample), and exactly one of our 21 (\gaia\ DR3 1450782713560551808) coincides with a star in Geha+26's broader $P_{\rm mem} > 0.5$ catalog --- specifically one of the four stars Geha+26 exclude from the $\sigma_v$ sample by the velocity-variable cut.  To cross-check Geha+26's reported dispersion on their own data, we fit a two-parameter Gaussian likelihood --- the same model Geha+26 use \citep[Walker2006 form;][]{Walker2006} --- on their 16-star $P_{\rm mem,novar} > 0.5$ sample with flat priors on $(\bar v_{\rm hel}, \sigma_v)$, sampled with \texttt{emcee}.  We recover $\bar v_{\rm hel} = 189.97^{+1.73}_{-1.90}$~\vlosUnits\ and $\sigma_v = 5.24^{+2.09}_{-1.68}$~\vlosUnits, in essentially exact agreement with the \citet{Geha2026} published values ($189.9 \pm 1.8$~\vlosUnits\ and $5.27^{+2.10}_{-1.67}$~\vlosUnits).  Repeating the same fit on the broader $P_{\rm mem} > 0.5$ sample of 19 stars (excluding the single RR~Lyrae cross-match) yields $\sigma_v = 7.60^{+1.97}_{-1.51}$~\vlosUnits, demonstrating that the inclusion of velocity-variable stars inflates the dispersion --- and consistent with the larger $\sigma_v \approx 10.7$~\vlosUnits\ obtained by \citet{Carlin2018} using a comparable single-mask sample without multi-epoch velocity-variable identification.  The residual difference between our $\sigma_v = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$~\vlosUnits\ and the \citet{Geha2026} $5.27$~\vlosUnits\ is therefore largely a sample-composition effect.  Directly testing this on Geha+26's data, the Walker2006 fit on the 11 RGB-only members of their $P_{\rm mem,novar} > 0.5$ sample (i.e.\ dropping the 5 BHB-flagged stars) yields $\sigma_v = 4.61^{+3.42}_{-2.64}$~\vlosUnits, somewhat smaller than the full 16-star value of $5.24$~\vlosUnits\ but with overlapping posteriors, indicating that the 5 BHB stars do slightly inflate the sample dispersion in their catalog --- consistent with BHB stars sampling a slightly different sub-population (or contributing additional kinematic structure beyond the bound RGB core).  The two surveys are also fully disjoint at $2''$ matching (0 overlap with Geha+26's 16-star $\sigma_v$ sample; 1 overlap with their broader $P_{\rm mem} > 0.5$ catalog, on one of the four stars they exclude as velocity-variable), and \SSSSS\ is brighter on average than Geha+26's DEIMOS sample, so the two datasets sample largely non-overlapping populations of Boo~III. In both \citet{Carlin2018} and \citet{Geha2026}, $\sigma_v$ is sensitive to the specific membership-classification procedure and the per-star RV-error model used.  Our use of a GMM with explicit per-star errors, weakly informative priors, and a joint $(v_{\rm hel}, [\mathrm{Fe/H}], \boldsymbol\mu)$ likelihood is designed to avoid biasing $\sigma_v$ upward through marginal contaminants.

For metallicity, our value is consistent with \citet{Carlin2018} within the joint uncertainties but with smaller errors. The \SSSSS\ DR2 RVs and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ are more recent and more precise than the legacy Hectospec data.  For proper motion, our values are consistent with both the \gaia~DR2-based measurement of \citet{Carlin2018} and the \gaia~DR3-based measurement of \citet{Pace2022} within the joint uncertainties; the agreement with Pace+22 is tighter at $\Delta\mu_\alpha\cos\delta \approx 0.005$~\pmUnits\ and $\Delta\mu_\delta \approx 0.007$~\pmUnits, because both works use \gaia\ DR3 (versus Carlin+18's DR2-based offsets of $0.02$ and $0.10$~\pmUnits, both still inside their $\sim 0.2$~\pmUnits\ errors). The combination of \SSSSS\ DR2 RVs/$[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ and \gaia\ DR3 proper motions yields a more reliable systemic description of Boo~III than was previously available.

% NOTE: this paragraph anticipates the tidal-disruption interpretation, fleshed out in Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}.  Keep here as a short comparison only.
The updated $\sigma_v$ resolves a previously puzzling discrepancy with other tidally disrupting dwarfs. With $\sigma_{v} = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$ \vlosUnits, Boo III now fits more naturally alongside Antlia~2 ($\sigma_v = 5.98$~\vlosUnits; \citealt{Ji2021}) and Crater~2 ($\sigma_v = 2.51$~\vlosUnits; \citealt{Limberg2025}) as examples of ultra-faint dwarfs with low velocity dispersions. The small velocity dispersion, combined with Boo III's recent pericentric passage ($\sim 0.13$ Gyr ago) and polar orbit, strongly supports the interpretation that Boo III is currently undergoing active tidal disruption. The implications of this low $\sigma_v$ for the system's dynamical state are discussed in Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}.


\section{Heliocentric Distance to Boötes~III} \label{section:distance}

The heliocentric distance to Boo~III has been measured several times since its discovery in SDSS by \citet{Grillmair2009}, who reported a CMD-based estimate of $\sim 46$~kpc. \citet{Carlin2009} adopted that distance for their initial spectroscopic study. \citet{Correnti2009} subsequently used a red-clump fit to SDSS photometry to derive an independent distance modulus $(m-M)_0 = 18.58 \pm 0.15$, corresponding to $d = 52.0 \pm 3.6$~kpc --- roughly 6~kpc larger than the CMD-based estimate. \citet{Sesar2014} then identified an RR~Lyrae star coincident with the Boo~III field whose distance, anchored to an $M_R = 0.6$ absolute-magnitude calibration in the Palomar Transient Factory (PTF) $R$-band, is $46 \pm 2$~kpc (a 6\% fractional precision; their Table~1 footnote) and agrees with the lower (\citealt{Grillmair2009}) value. The currently adopted value, $d = 46.5 \pm 2.0$~kpc, comes from the analysis of \citet{Carlin2018}, who performed a least-squares fit of the M15 horizontal-branch ridgeline (with M15 taken as the metallicity analogue of Boo~III, $[{\rm Fe/H}] = -2.34$) to the Boo~III blue horizontal-branch (BHB) stars in PanSTARRS-1 photometry. The formal best-fit distance modulus is $(m-M)_0 = 18.34 \pm 0.02$, but \citet{Carlin2018} conservatively inflate this to $\pm 2$~kpc to account for the small BHB sample and the uncertainty on the M15 distance itself. \citet{Pace2022} adopt this value, while \citet{Moskowitz2020} adopt the \citet{Correnti2009} red-clump value of $52 \pm 3.6$~kpc directly. The $\sim 6$~kpc tension between the red-clump route (Correnti) and the RRL/isochrone route (Sesar, Carlin) is at the $\sim 1.5\sigma$ level and is unresolved.

We also derive an independent distance to Boo~III from five \gaia~DR3 RR~Lyrae stars associated with the system (Table~\ref{tab:rrl_distances}): the three RRLs identified directly in the \SSSSS\ footprint via the box-cut and Gaia DR3 RRL catalog cross-match of Section~\ref{section:membership}, plus one additional RRL match each in the published \citet{Carlin2018} and \citet{Geha2026} spectroscopic member samples. For each RRL we use the intensity-averaged \gaia~$G$ magnitude (\texttt{int\_average\_g}). For the extinction we adopt the \citet{Schlegel1998} $E(B-V)$ rescaled by the $0.86$ factor of \citet{Schlafly2011} and convert to $A_G$ using the colour-dependent extinction law of \citet{Babusiaux2018}; the resulting $A_G$ values are in the range 0.034--0.048~mag (with an effective $R_G \equiv A_G/E(B-V)\simeq 2.7$), an order of magnitude larger than the GSP-Phot \texttt{ag\_gspphot} values, which are known to be biased low for hot, metal-poor stars. The absolute magnitude is computed from the period-independent $M_G$--$[{\rm Fe/H}]$ calibration of \citet{Garofalo2022} (their Eq.~19, anchored to the Zinn \& West 1984 metallicity scale): $M_G = (0.33 \pm 0.02)\,[{\rm Fe/H}] + (1.05 \pm 0.03)$, with hierarchical-Bayesian intrinsic dispersion $\sigma_M = 0.03$~mag. Evaluated at Boo~III's system-mean $[{\rm Fe/H}] = -2.34$ (the per-RRL spectroscopic $[{\rm Fe/H}]$ is unreliable due to pulsation-phase broadening of the equivalent widths), this gives $M_G = +0.28$~mag. The individual distance moduli (Table~\ref{tab:rrl_distances}) are tightly clustered, with an internal scatter of only $0.05$~mag.

The mean across the five stars yields $\langle m-M \rangle = 18.43 \pm 0.09$, corresponding to $\langle d \rangle = 48.5 \pm 1.9$~kpc.  The five per-star distances span 47.6--50.0~kpc with an internal scatter of $1.0$~kpc, consistent with the per-star statistical uncertainty.  At the mean level, the dominant contribution is the correlated systematic ($\sigma_{m-M}^{\rm stat} = 0.123/\sqrt{5} = 0.055$~mag, $\sigma_{m-M}^{\rm sys} = 0.066$~mag).  The dominant per-star statistical term ($0.119$~mag) is the propagation of the galaxy-wide $[{\rm Fe/H}]$ dispersion $\sigma_{[{\rm Fe/H}]} = 0.36$~dex through the calibration slope (each RRL is an independent draw from the galaxy [Fe/H] distribution, so the term reduces as $1/\sqrt{N}$); adding the calibration's intrinsic-dispersion contribution $\sigma_M = 0.03$~mag in quadrature gives the total per-star statistical $0.123$~mag.  The systematic budget combines three terms that are shared by all five RRLs and do not reduce with $N$: the slope-times-$[{\rm Fe/H}]$ term ($\sigma_{\rm slope}\,|[{\rm Fe/H}]| = 0.02 \times 2.34 = 0.047$~mag), the intercept uncertainty ($\sigma_{\rm intercept} = 0.030$~mag), and the propagation of the uncertainty on the galaxy-mean $[{\rm Fe/H}]$ through the slope (${\rm slope} \times \sigma_{\overline{[{\rm Fe/H}]}} = 0.33 \times 0.11 = 0.036$~mag), which sum in quadrature to $\sqrt{0.047^2 + 0.030^2 + 0.036^2} = 0.066$~mag. For comparison, repeating the same analysis with the earlier \citet{Muraveva2018} G-band calibration based on Gaia DR2 ($M_G = (0.32 \pm 0.04)\,[{\rm Fe/H}] + (1.11 \pm 0.06)$, intrinsic dispersion $\sigma_{\rm int} = 0.17$~mag) yields $\langle m-M \rangle_{\rm Mur18} = 18.3 \pm 0.2$, $\langle d \rangle_{\rm Mur18} = 46.6 \pm 3.6$~kpc. This is consistent with the \citet{Garofalo2022} value within their joint uncertainty, and agrees almost exactly with the $46.5 \pm 2.0$~kpc value of \citet{Carlin2018} (based on the M15 horizontal-branch ridgeline fit to Boo~III BHB stars described above). The agreement with \citet{Carlin2018} despite the very different anchor (M15 distance modulus versus an RRL period--luminosity--metallicity relation) is a coincidence; the more direct comparison is between the two RRL calibrations, where the $\sim 1.9$~kpc offset to \citet{Garofalo2022} reflects the move from a DR2-based calibration in \citet{Muraveva2018} to an EDR3-based calibration in \citet{Garofalo2022}. The DR2-to-EDR3 shift corresponds to a $\sim 0.08$~mag systematic offset in $M_G$ between the two calibrations and places the EDR3-based value roughly half-way between \citet{Carlin2018} and the $52.0 \pm 3.6$~kpc red-clump value of \citet{Correnti2009} adopted by \citet{Moskowitz2020}, agreeing with both at $\lesssim 1\sigma$. We adopt the \citet{Garofalo2022}-based 5-RRL value, $d = 48.5 \pm 1.9$~kpc, throughout the present analysis as it incorporates the more recent EDR3 parallax solution and has both tighter statistical and systematic precision than \citet{Muraveva2018}.



\section{Orbit and Stream Simulations} 

\subsection{Orbit} \label{section:orbit}
We integrate the orbit of Boo~III using the 6D phase-space parameters: the systemic velocity and proper motion measured in Section~\ref{section:membership}, combined with the sky position and heliocentric distance listed in Table~\ref{tab:params}. The integration is performed with the \texttt{galpy} Python package \citep{Bovy2015}, adopting the \texttt{McMillan17} potential \citep{McMillan2017}. This potential assumes a Solar radius of $R_0 = 8.21$~kpc and circular velocity of $V_0 = 233.1$ \vlosUnits. We adopt the Solar peculiar motion $(U,V,W)_\odot = (-12.9, +12.5, +7.78)$ \vlosUnits chosen so that the Sun's total velocity in the Galactocentric frame matches the \texttt{astropy}~v4.0 default $(V_x, V_y, V_z)_\odot = (12.9, 245.6, 7.78)$ \vlosUnits \citep{Drimmel2018}, giving a total Solar velocity of $V_{\phi,\odot} = 245.6$ \vlosUnits with respect to the Galactic center. The same convention is used for the LMC orbit integration described below.

We include the gravitational influence of the LMC, which induces a reflex motion of the MW barycenter that must be accounted for in the orbit integration \citep{Erkal2019, Petersen2021}. We model the LMC as a moving Hernquist potential \citep{Hernquist1990} added to the \texttt{McMillan17} base potential, and include the resulting non-inertial frame correction to account for the MW's barycentric acceleration.

We adopt the LMC parameters from \citet{Pace2022}: a total mass of $1.38 \times 10^{11}~\mathrm{M}_\odot$ and an enclosed mass of $1.7 \times 10^{10}~\mathrm{M}_\odot$ at 8.7~kpc \citep{vanderMarel2014}.  Solving the Hernquist (1990) $M(<r) = M_{\rm tot}\,r^2/(r+a)^2$ relation for these values yields a scale radius $a = 16.09$~kpc and a half-mass radius $r_{\rm hm} = a(1+\sqrt{2}) = 38.84$~kpc, which we adopt in the LMC potential below.

Figure~\ref{fig:orbits} shows the resulting orbits integrated 4~Gyr backwards, with and without the LMC. To propagate observational uncertainties into the inferred orbital parameters, we generate 1000 Monte-Carlo realisations of the 6D phase-space coordinates by drawing each component independently from a Gaussian distribution centred on the median value and with width equal to the $1\sigma$ uncertainty listed in Table~\ref{tab:params}. The pericenter, apocenter, and eccentricity are taken as the median of these realisations, with the 16th and 84th percentiles as uncertainties (Figure~\ref{fig:orbit_params}). Including the LMC reduces the apocenter and slightly increases the pericenter.

We also present the orbital parameters in Table~\ref{tab:params}.

As seen in Figure~\ref{fig:orbits}, the motion of Boo~III is largely confined to the Galactocentric $YZ$-plane, confirming the polar orbit found by \citet{Carlin2018}. In the static MW potential, Boo~III passed its most recent pericenter $0.130 \pm 0.008$~Gyr ago at $r_{\rm peri} \approx 9.0^{+2.1}_{-1.8}$~kpc; including the LMC gives a similar time of $0.135 \pm 0.008$~Gyr ago at $r_{\rm peri} \approx 9.5^{+2.1}_{-1.8}$~kpc (Table~\ref{tab:params}).  Both the small pericentre distance and the recent passage are essential for the tidal-disruption interpretation discussed in Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}: a wider orbit would never feel a strong instantaneous tidal field, and a system whose most recent pericentre was many Gyr ago would have relaxed back to a quasi-equilibrium configuration. The primary effect of the LMC is to reduce the apocenter distance (Figure~\ref{fig:orbit_params}), confining Boo~III to a tighter orbit.

For comparison, we repeat the orbit integration using the lighter \texttt{MWPotential2014} \citep{Bovy2015}, which yields markedly larger pericenter, apocenter, and eccentricity: with the LMC included, the median orbital parameters shift from $(r_{\rm peri}, r_{\rm apo}, e) = (9.5~\mathrm{kpc}, 97~\mathrm{kpc}, 0.82)$ in the \texttt{McMillan17} potential to $(12.4~\mathrm{kpc}, 163~\mathrm{kpc}, 0.86)$ in \texttt{MWPotential2014}. The apocenter therefore nearly doubles --- and the orbital period correspondingly lengthens from $\sim 1.5$~Gyr to $\sim 3$~Gyr --- when the lighter MW model is used, although the qualitative character of the orbit (polar, retrograde, highly eccentric, with a recent pericentric passage) is unchanged. The corresponding orbital trajectories and pericentre/apocentre posteriors are shown in Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:orbits_mwp2014} and \ref{fig:orbit_params_mwp2014}. The per-component propagation of the 6D phase-space uncertainties into the inferred orbital quantities is presented in Appendix~\ref{section:appendix_6dmc}, which identifies the heliocentric distance as the dominant uncertainty driver.


\subsection{Stream}\label{section:stream}
To model the tidal stream expected from Boo~III's disruption, we use the particle spray code \texttt{fardal15spraydf} in \texttt{galpy} \citep{Bovy2015}, an implementation of the \citet{Fardal2015} spray model.  This method integrates the progenitor's orbit from a chosen disruption time to the present, continuously releasing particles at the L1 and L2 Lagrange points to build up a mock stream.  We defer the description of the Lagrange-point release physics and its quantitative implications to Section~\ref{section:gradient} (in connection with the velocity-gradient interpretation).

The input parameters are the progenitor mass, the 6D phase-space coordinates, the disruption time, and the Galactic potential. For the progenitor mass we adopt the \citet{Wolf2010} half-light dynamical mass $M_{1/2} \approx 1.24 \times 10^{6}\,M_\odot$ derived in Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt} (Eq.~\ref{eq:Mhalf}), which combines our GMM line-of-sight dispersion with the literature angular size and our new RR~Lyrae distance.  We adopt a disruption time of 3~Gyr and use the same MW+LMC potential as in the orbit integration. All fiducial stream parameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:streamspray_params}.

\input{streamparams_table}\label{tab:streamspray_params}

To study the sensitivity of the predicted stream to the assumed potential, we generate several variations by changing one parameter at a time relative to the fiducial model:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=0pt]
    \item LMC mass halved, doubled, or removed from potential entirely
    \item MW halo mass halved, or increased by a factor of 1.5, or doubled
    \item Solar velocity decreased to $V_\phi = 233$ \vlosUnits, or increased to $V_\phi = 258$ \vlosUnits
\end{itemize}
Figure~\ref{fig:stream_comparison} compares the fiducial particle-spray model and these one-parameter-at-a-time variations to the on-sky Styx stream track from \citet{Grillmair2009} (the track data themselves are not published in that paper and were supplied by C.~Grillmair, priv.~comm.). The fiducial (\texttt{McMillan17}~+~LMC) prediction lies close to but slightly south of Styx, with similar overall trends in proper motion. The three one-parameter scans then show how the predicted stream moves with: (i) the Milky Way halo mass (column 2: $\times 0.5$, $\times 1$, $\times 1.5$, $\times 2$ of \texttt{McMillan17}); (ii) the LMC mass (column 3: no-LMC, $\times 0.5$, $\times 1$, $\times 2$ of the fiducial value); and (iii) the Sun's circular speed $V_\phi$ (column 4: $233$~\vlosUnits, fiducial $245.6$~\vlosUnits, $258$~\vlosUnits). The behaviour is qualitatively as expected: a lighter MW halo, a heavier LMC, or a smaller solar $V_\phi$ all bring the predicted Boo~III stream closer to the observed Styx track, while the opposite changes pull it away. Of the five observables, declination and proper motion are partially degenerate between the three perturbations, but the radial-velocity track --- particularly within $\pm 10^\circ$ of Boo~III in RA --- separates the model variations most cleanly.

The simulated streams follow tracks broadly consistent with the Styx stream, providing additional support for the Boo~III--Styx connection beyond the proper motion alignment noted by \citet{Carlin2018}. \section{Discussion} \label{section:discussion}
\subsection{Tidal Disruption of Boötes~III} \label{section:tidaldisrupt}


We assess whether Boo~III is currently undergoing tidal disruption using three complementary diagnostics, presented in the order in which we treat them below: (i) the timing and geometry of its most recent pericentric passage (Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}, ``Recent pericentric passage''); (ii) its tidal radius at pericentre, computed from the half-light dynamical mass and the enclosed Milky-Way mass profile (``Tidal radius far smaller than the half-light radius''); and (iii) the equivalent density comparison of its mean half-light density against the enclosed Milky-Way density at pericentre (``Density below the Milky Way envelope''). All three independently favour active tidal stripping, and we discuss them in turn.

\subsubsection{Recent pericentric passage} Section~\ref{section:orbit} found that Boo~III's last pericentric passage was just $\sim 0.13$~Gyr ago, at a Galactocentric distance of $r_{\mathrm{peri}} \approx 9.5$~kpc (with LMC; Table~\ref{tab:params}).  This is a small pericentre for a satellite at the present-day Galactocentric distance of $\sim 50$~kpc, and a very recent passage compared with Boo~III's $\sim 1.5$~Gyr orbital period in the \texttt{McMillan17}~+~LMC integration. Both are necessary preconditions for an actively-stripping picture: a system with a substantially larger pericentre would never experience a strong instantaneous tidal field, and a system whose last pericentric passage occurred many Gyr ago would by now have had time to relax back to a quasi-equilibrium configuration.

\subsubsection{Tidal radius far smaller than the half-light radius}
The tidal radius for a satellite on a circular orbit in a host with a flat rotation curve \citep{King1962} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rt}
    r_t = r \left( \frac{m}{2 M(<r)} \right)^{1/3},
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the satellite's distance from the Milky Way, $m$ is the satellite mass, and $M(<r)$ is the enclosed Milky Way mass.  We evaluate Equation~\ref{eq:rt} for Boo~III at its most recent pericentre. The two ingredients are the satellite mass within its half-light radius and the Milky Way mass enclosed at the pericentric distance.

For the satellite, we adopt the half-light dynamical mass from Table~\ref{tab:params}, computed via the \citet{Wolf2010} estimator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Mhalf}
    M_{\rm half} \;=\; 930 \, \sigma_v^2 \, r_h
    \;\approx\; 1.24 \times 10^{6}\,M_\odot,
\end{equation}
where the constant $930$ has units of $M_\odot \, \rm pc^{-1} \, (km \, s^{-1})^{-2}$, and we use our mixture-model line-of-sight dispersion $\sigma_v = 1.69$~\vlosUnits\ (Section~\ref{section:membership}) together with the (circularised) half-light radius $r_h = 466$~pc (from the \citet{Moskowitz2020} angular size combined with our updated distance of $48.5$~kpc; see Table~\ref{tab:params}), matching the convention of \citet{Pace2022}. This $M_{1/2}$ value is the canonical half-light mass used throughout the paper, including as the progenitor mass for the particle-spray stream model of Section~\ref{section:stream}.

For the Milky Way enclosed mass we use the spherically equivalent value
\begin{equation}
    M(<r) \;=\; v_c^2(r)\,r/G,
\end{equation}
evaluated in the static \texttt{McMillan17} potential. Although the orbit integration in Section~\ref{section:orbit} also includes the LMC's reflex acceleration on the Milky Way, the LMC perturbation is small near the inner Galaxy and the local circular velocity at $r \sim 9$~kpc is well described by \texttt{McMillan17} alone. Adopting the with-LMC pericentric distance from Section~\ref{section:orbit}, $r_{\mathrm{peri}} = 9.47$~kpc, we obtain $v_c(r_{\mathrm{peri}}) = 233$~\vlosUnits\ and $M(<r_{\mathrm{peri}}) \approx 1.20 \times 10^{11}\,M_\odot$.

Substituting into Equation~\ref{eq:rt} yields $r_t \approx 164$~pc at pericenter, or $\approx 12'$ on the sky at Boo~III's distance, which is $\sim 0.3\,r_h$ --- about a factor of three smaller than the half-light radius. The dominant uncertainty is the velocity dispersion: propagating the $1\sigma$ range $\sigma_v = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$~\vlosUnits\ alone gives $r_t \approx 100$--$225$~pc ($\approx 7'$--$16'$, or $0.2$--$0.5\,r_h$), all substantially smaller than $r_h$. We caution that this estimate assumes Boo~III is in dynamical equilibrium and uses the instantaneous tidal field at pericenter; both assumptions are clearly violated for an actively disrupting system, so $r_t$ should be regarded as an order-of-magnitude estimate of the bound-region size at closest approach.

\subsubsection{Density below the Milky Way envelope}
The result $r_t < r_h$ for Boo~III can also be phrased as a density condition.  When the half-light radius equals the tidal radius, Equation~\ref{eq:rt} reduces to $\bar\rho_{1/2} = 2\,\bar\rho_{\mathrm{MW}}(r_{\mathrm{peri}})$, i.e.\ a satellite is expected to be significantly affected by tidal stripping when its average internal density falls below twice the mean enclosed Milky Way density at its orbital pericenter \citep{Pace2022}. \fixme{Andrew, please check}  For Boo~III we find $\bar\rho_{\rm half}/[2\bar\rho_{\mathrm{MW}}(r_{\mathrm{peri}})] \approx 0.04$, well below unity and consistent with the position of Boo~III in Figure~\ref{fig:pericenter_density}.  This figure, adapted from Figure~5 (left panel, with LMC) of \citet{Pace2022} with Boo~III updated to our new measurements, makes the key impact of our revised velocity dispersion immediately apparent.  With the previous measurement of $\sigma_v = 10.7 \pm 3.5$~\vlosUnits\ \citep{Carlin2018}, Boo~III sits above the MW density line, suggesting that its internal density is sufficient to resist tidal disruption despite its small pericenter.  With our updated value of $\sigma_v = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$~\vlosUnits, the implied dynamical mass---and hence the average density---decreases dramatically, placing Boo~III well below the line.

This result places Boo~III among the clearly tidally disrupting satellites in the MW system, alongside Antlia~2, Crater~2, Sagittarius, and Tucana~III. The combination of a small pericenter ($r_{\mathrm{peri}} \sim 9.5$\,kpc), a very recent pericentric passage ($\sim 0.13$\,Gyr ago), and a low average density is fully consistent with Boo~III being in a state of active tidal disruption. Additionally, the low density and small pericenter of Boo~III are consistent with its puffy, extended structure relative to other dSphs of similar luminosity, as tidal heating can inflate a satellite's half-light radius during close pericentric passages.

\subsection{Velocity Gradient and Stream Prediction} \label{section:gradient}

To probe whether the observed kinematics deviate from a bound, dispersion-supported equilibrium and to make falsifiable predictions for the predicted Boo~III tidal stream, we measure the on-sky velocity gradient of the 21 GMM members and compare it to three independent model predictions: a solid-body / perspective-rotation expectation, an orbit-based prediction, and a self-consistent $N$-body simulation.  

\subsubsection{N-body simulation setup}
We examine $N$-body simulations of a Boo~III-like progenitor evolved on Boo~III's present-day orbit, generated using the same approach and code as the Boo~I simulation of \citet{Sandford2025}. Briefly, each run uses \texttt{GADGET-3} \citep[an improved version of the \texttt{GADGET-2} code of][]{Springel2005} to evolve a Plummer stellar component embedded in a Navarro--Frenk--White (NFW) dark-matter halo through a combined Milky Way + LMC gravitational potential. Initial conditions are generated with \texttt{AGAMA} \citep{Vasiliev2019}: we rewind a tracer particle from Boo~III's present-day phase-space coordinates to its past apocenter ($\sim 4$~Gyr lookback, well before the most recent pericentric passage) \fixme{[needs check against the IC-generation script]}, place the simulated progenitor there, and integrate forward through the same MW+LMC potential to the present day. The MW is modelled with \texttt{McMillan17} \citep{McMillan2017}, matching the potential adopted for the orbit integration of Section~\ref{section:orbit} and the particle-spray models of Section~\ref{section:stream}, and the LMC with a Hernquist profile of mass $1.5\times10^{11}\,M_\odot$ and scale radius $17.13$~kpc, with present-day phase-space coordinates from \citet{Kallivayalil2013}, \citet{Pietrzynski2019}, and \citet{vandermarel2002}, and dynamical friction modelled following \citet{Jethwa2016}. \fixme{Denis, these numbers here are different from section 5.1. I set the section 5.1 numbers for lmc so that it match with 
Pace+2022. Let me know if you think I should rerun everything to match the numbers here.} The Plummer stellar component is set up to roughly match Boo~III's observed luminosity ($M_V = -5.8$); we do not attempt a precise match to the structural parameters since the simulated Plummer profile evolves under tides during the integration, and the present-day projected size of the bound remnant in the simulation differs from the literature half-light radius.

We perform three simulations that differ only in the present-day NFW dark-matter halo mass: $M_{\rm DM} = 10^6$, $10^7$, and $10^8\,M_\odot$ \fixme{[NFW concentration / scale radius / virial mass placeholders carried over from the Boo~I setup; need to be confirmed against the IC-generation script.]}. The $10^8\,M_\odot$ value is the canonical halo mass for ultra-faint dwarfs in $\Lambda$CDM \citep[e.g.,][]{Simon2019} and is roughly the minimum mass at which a galaxy can form under standard models of galaxy formation; the $10^7$ and $10^6\,M_\odot$ runs represent progressively stripped progenitors. As we show in Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt_vdisp} below, only the $10^6\,M_\odot$ run reproduces our observed line-of-sight velocity dispersion. We therefore adopt the $10^6\,M_\odot$ simulation as the fiducial run for all subsequent figures and gradient comparisons in this section, with the $10^7$ and $10^8\,M_\odot$ runs retained only for the dispersion comparison of Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt_vdisp}.

\subsubsection{Velocity-dispersion comparison} \label{section:tidaldisrupt_vdisp}
We measure the line-of-sight velocity dispersion within the 3 $r_h$ ellipse for each of the three simulations and find a clear monotonic trend: $\sigma_v = 1.92$~\vlosUnits\ at $M_{\rm DM} = 10^6\,M_\odot$, $\sigma_v = 3.07$~\vlosUnits\ at $10^7\,M_\odot$, and $\sigma_v = 5.31$~\vlosUnits\ at $10^8\,M_\odot$. Comparing against our observed $\sigma_v = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$~\vlosUnits\ (Section~\ref{section:membership}), only the $10^6\,M_\odot$ run is consistent with the data; the $10^7\,M_\odot$ run is marginally inconsistent, and the $10^8\,M_\odot$ run is incompatible at the $\gtrsim 4\sigma$ level (even within the bound core $r \lesssim 0.75\,r_h$ the $10^8\,M_\odot$ dispersion remains $\sigma_v \approx 5$~\vlosUnits). However, $10^6\,M_\odot$ is too low a halo mass to host an in-situ galaxy under standard $\Lambda$CDM galaxy-formation models, so this cannot represent the progenitor's birth halo. The natural interpretation is that Boo~III formed in a halo of order $10^8\,M_\odot$ (consistent with CDM expectations for a UFD of its luminosity), but has since lost a substantial fraction of its dark-matter mass to tidal stripping, leaving the present-day surviving bound mass much smaller than the natal halo mass. The observed low $\sigma_v$ thus tracks the much-reduced bound mass that remains, in line with the actively-disrupting picture of Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}.

\subsubsection{Velocity-gradient comparison}
Following \citet{Sandford2025}, we distinguish between the \emph{observed} line-of-sight velocity gradient $\nabla v_{\rm obs}$ measured directly from member stars on the sky and the \emph{intrinsic} gradient that remains after removing the geometric contribution from perspective rotation. For a system at distance $d$ moving with bulk proper motion $\boldsymbol{\mu}$, the projection of the systemic transverse velocity onto the line of sight changes with sky position, producing an apparent gradient even for a bound, dispersion-supported system with no internal rotation. The amplitude of this perspective contribution scales as $|\Delta v_{\rm persp}| \approx 4.74\,|\boldsymbol{\mu}|\,d\,\theta$ (with $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ in mas yr$^{-1}$, $d$ in kpc, and angular separation $\theta$ in radians) and is aligned with the apparent proper-motion vector.

To measure the observed gradient, we refit the 21 high-probability members of Section~\ref{section:membership} with an 8-parameter model that augments the same Gaussian likelihood with two linear gradient components ($\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\xi$, $\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\eta$); the eight parameters are $v_{\rm hel,0}$, $\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\xi$, $\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\eta$, $\sigma_v$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]_0$, $\sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}$, $\mu_{\alpha,0}\cos\delta$, and $\mu_{\delta,0}$ (i.e.\ the seven galaxy parameters of the Section~\ref{section:membership} GMM with the mixture fraction dropped, since this fit is performed directly on the 21-member sample). We obtain $|\nabla v|_{\rm obs} = 3.20^{+1.6}_{-1.7}$~\vlosUnits~deg$^{-1}$ at $\mathrm{PA} = 169^\circ$ (yellow point in Figure~\ref{fig:fig7_gradient}, top-right panel; the corresponding sky-panel arrow is shown in yellow in the top-left panel).

We compare this measurement against three model predictions, all shown in the top-right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:fig7_gradient}. The \emph{solid-body / perspective rotation prediction} (red square) is computed by assuming the 21 members move rigidly at the GMM-fit progenitor velocity and projecting their predicted heliocentric $v_{\rm los}$ onto the sky; for Boo~III's $d = 48.5$~kpc and $|\boldsymbol{\mu}| = 1.46$~\pmUnits\ this predicts $(\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\xi,\,\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\eta) = (-4.63, -3.57)$~\vlosUnits~deg$^{-1}$, $|\nabla v|_{\rm persp} = 5.85$~\vlosUnits~deg$^{-1}$ at $\mathrm{PA} \approx 232^\circ$. The \emph{orbit prediction} (diamond markers, viridis-coloured by halo-mass scaling factor) is the local linear $v_{\rm los}$ slope along the integrated McMillan17~+~LMC orbit at the present-day position, evaluated for halo masses scaled by $f \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.25, 1.5\}$ relative to fiducial McMillan17. We use the smooth progenitor orbit's local $v_{\rm los}$ slope as the gradient reference here, rather than a direct 2-D least-squares fit to the spray particles within a few degrees of the progenitor, because spray particles near the centre are dominated by the stochastic Lagrange-point release (the Jacobi radius is $r_J \approx 0.5$~kpc near the present-day position), whereas the bulk stream cleanly traces the smooth progenitor orbit (Figure~\ref{fig:phi1_stream}). Finally, the \emph{N-body simulation prediction} (cyan triangle) is the linear gradient measured from the 123{,}201 particles within $2\,r_h$ in our fiducial $10^6\,M_\odot$ Boo~III simulation (integrated in the same \texttt{McMillan17}~+~LMC potential as the spray and orbit models; Section~\ref{section:tidaldisrupt}): $(\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\xi,\,\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\eta) = (+1.61, -1.03)$~\vlosUnits~deg$^{-1}$, $|\nabla v|_{\rm simulation} = 1.91$~\vlosUnits~deg$^{-1}$ at $\mathrm{PA} = 122^\circ$ (the bottom-left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:fig7_gradient} shows the underlying mean-$v_{\rm los}$ map and the corresponding cyan arrow within $2\,r_h$).

The observed gradient lies between the perspective-rotation expectation and the simulation/orbit predictions, broadly consistent with a partially-disrupted system in which the bound core (dominated by perspective rotation, with a near-zero intrinsic gradient as for Boo~I in \citealt{Sandford2025}) is mixed with tidally stripped stars whose motions deviate from rigid-body. The bottom-right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:fig7_gradient} shows the line-of-sight velocity dispersion of the same simulation particles within $2\,r_h$; the median of $\sim 1.7$~\vlosUnits\ matches the observed $\sigma_v$, supporting the interpretation that the inner, bound region of Boo~III is reasonably well-described by the $10^6\,M_\odot$ progenitor simulation. With only 21 members, however, the observed gradient remains statistically degenerate with both the perspective-only and simulation predictions; a definitive separation will require a larger and deeper member sample.

\subsubsection{$\phi_1$ stream prediction}
Beyond the on-sky velocity gradient diagnostic, our models also make joint predictions of $v_{\rm los}$ and heliocentric distance along the predicted stream direction. By construction, $\phi_1 = 0$ at Boo~III's centre, and positive $\phi_1$ points along the on-sky heliocentric direction of motion (i.e.\ toward the \emph{leading} tail, which sits ahead of the progenitor on its orbit; spray particles in the leading arm satisfy $\phi_1 > 0$ in our integration, as is evident from the spray-particle distribution in Figure~\ref{fig:phi1_stream}).

\subsection{Comparison with Typhon stream}
The Typhon stream was discovered as a dynamically coherent group of metal-poor halo stars on a highly eccentric, polar orbit by \citet{Tenachi2022}  and characterised further in subsequent \gaia-based and spectroscopic analyses. Like Boo~III, Typhon's polar orbit and high eccentricity suggest that it is the debris of a tidally disrupted progenitor on a similar dynamical trajectory. The similarities make Typhon a natural comparison case for assessing whether Boo~III shares its dynamical neighbourhood and possibly its infall history. To investigate this potential connection, we compare their orbital parameters and examine whether they occupy overlapping regions in action and integrals-of-motion space.

We compute the properties of stars from the Typhon stream and compare with those from other dwarf galaxies. Specifically, we use the action diamond, energy vs. angular momentum, and apocenter vs. pericenter parameter spaces. For the 6D parameters of Boo~III we use the values derived in this work (Table~\ref{tab:params}); for all other dwarf galaxies in the comparison sample we adopt the values compiled in the Local Volume Database (LVDB; \citealt{Pace2025}, \url{https://github.com/apace7/local_volume_database}).

We again use the \texttt{galpy} Python package for these calculations, in the \texttt{McMillan17} potential. For each input 6D phase-space coordinate we instantiate a \texttt{galpy} \texttt{Orbit}, integrate it forward by 10~Gyr, and read off the most recent pericenter and apocenter from the integrated radial distance. The three action components $(J_R, L_z, J_z)$ and the total energy $E$ are obtained directly from the \texttt{Orbit} object's built-in methods (\texttt{Orbit.jr}, \texttt{Orbit.jp}, \texttt{Orbit.jz}, \texttt{Orbit.E}, and \texttt{Orbit.Lz}), which evaluate the actions using the Staeckel-fudge approximation \citep{Binney2012Staeckel} in the same \texttt{McMillan17} potential. For each dwarf galaxy in the comparison sample we propagate its 6D uncertainties via 50 Monte-Carlo realisations (drawn from Gaussian distributions centred on the LVDB median values with widths equal to the quoted $1\sigma$ uncertainties) and take the median of those 50 realisations as the representative value plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:typhon_and_dwarfs}; for Boo~III we use our updated Table~\ref{tab:params} values. For the Typhon stream, every member star has its own measured 6D, so we compute its actions, energy and orbital extrema directly from the per-star measurements (without MC sampling) and plot each member individually as a black star in Figure~\ref{fig:typhon_and_dwarfs}.Figure~\ref{fig:typhon_and_dwarfs} shows the comparison of Boo~III and Typhon against the rest of the LVDB dwarf-galaxy sample. We additionally compute the same set of parameters for the other stellar streams in the \SSSSS catalogue (handled identically to Typhon: per-member values, no MC sampling). Figure~\ref{fig:typhon_and_streams} compares Boo~III and Typhon against this stream sample.

Of all the dwarfs in our comparison sample, Boo~III is the closest neighbour of Typhon in every projection we examined (the action diamond, $E$ vs.~$L_z$, and pericentre vs.~apocentre). Equivalently, of all the streams, Typhon is the closest neighbour of Boo~III. This dynamical proximity, combined with their shared polar, highly-eccentric character, is suggestive of a common-infall origin in which both systems entered the Milky Way potential together \citep[similar in spirit to other proposed accreted groups, e.g.][]{Ji2023Typhon}. However, Typhon's mean metallicity from high-resolution spectroscopy of 7 stars is $\overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]} = -1.69 \pm 0.11$ \citep{Ji2023Typhon} --- substantially higher than Boo~III's ($\overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]} = -2.34 \pm 0.11$). A direct progenitor--debris relationship --- e.g.\ Typhon being stripped material from the Boo~III progenitor --- is therefore disfavoured by the chemistry. The most natural interpretation is that the two systems are kinematically associated as members of the same accreted group but had distinct progenitors with different chemical-evolution histories before infall.

\subsection{Prospects for Detecting the Boo~III Tidal Tail} \label{section:tail_prospects}
Beyond the 21 GMM members within the 3 $r_h$ ellipse and the two candidate tidal-tail members within $\sim 7\,a_h$ of the centre that we identify in Appendix~\ref{section:appendix_tail}, a direct detection of an extended Boo~III tidal stream would provide an independent confirmation of the disruption picture and a powerful new constraint on the joint MW + LMC potential. We searched for such a stream using two distance-tracer populations from \gaia\ DR3: blue horizontal-branch (BHB) stars and RR~Lyrae (RRL) stars. In each case we selected candidates with sky positions and proper motions consistent with our fiducial particle-spray model from Section~\ref{section:stream}. Neither search yielded a convincing extended detection.

A key obstacle is contamination from the Sagittarius (Sgr) stream, which runs through the same patch of sky as the predicted Boo~III tidal tail. Figure~\ref{fig:sgr_and_boo} compares our fiducial Boo~III stream model with the Sgr-stream members from \citet{Vasiliev2021}. At the on-sky location of the predicted Boo~III tail, the two structures overlap in sky position, proper motion, \emph{and} heliocentric distance --- Sgr debris in this longitude range sits at $\sim 40$--$55$~kpc, essentially the same range as the predicted Boo~III tail --- so photometric distance tracers alone cannot separate them. Their radial velocities, however, differ by $\gtrsim 100$~\vlosUnits, providing a clean spectroscopic discriminant. The two streams' distance gradients also run in opposite directions: the Sgr leading arm decreases in distance with increasing R.A.\ in this region, while the predicted Boo~III tail increases (Section~\ref{section:gradient}). Once Boo~III members are identified spectroscopically, the opposite-sign distance gradient becomes an additional consistency check, but it cannot drive the initial detection.

We therefore conclude that a deep spectroscopic campaign along the predicted stream track --- using radial velocity as the primary discriminant against Sgr --- is the most promising path to confirming the Boo~III--Styx connection. Future wide-field multi-object spectroscopic (MOS) surveys (e.g.\ DESI, 4MOST, Subaru/PFS) targeting this region could definitively establish the physical association, and the spectroscopic 6D of the recovered tail members would simultaneously constrain the MW halo mass and the LMC mass via the gradient diagnostic of Section~\ref{section:gradient} and Appendix~\ref{section:appendix_6dmc}.


\section{Conclusion}\label{section:conclusion}

We have presented updated systemic properties of the ultra-faint dwarf galaxy \boo based on 24 member stars identified from \SSSSS DR2 spectroscopy and \gaia DR3 astrometry. Our main results are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

\item \textbf{Updated systemic properties, centroid, and heliocentric distance.} From a Gaussian-mixture-model fit, we measure a velocity dispersion of $\sigma_{v} = 1.69^{+1.03}_{-0.85}$ \vlosUnits, more than five times smaller than the $10.7 \pm 3.5$ \vlosUnits reported by \citet{Carlin2018}. This revised value brings Boo~III in line with other known tidally disrupting dwarfs such as Antlia~2 ($\sigma_v = 5.98$ \vlosUnits; \citealt{Ji2021}) and Crater~2 ($\sigma_v = 2.51$ \vlosUnits; \citealt{Limberg2025}). We also measure a systemic velocity of $v_{\mathrm{hel}} = 191.22^{+0.65}_{-0.80}$ \vlosUnits, proper motion $(\mu_{\alpha} \cos{\delta}, \mu_\delta) = (-1.163^{+0.017}_{-0.019}, -0.883^{+0.013}_{-0.014})$ \pmUnits, mean metallicity $\mathrm{[Fe/H]} = -2.34 \pm 0.11$~dex, and an updated spatial centroid $(\alpha_0, \delta_0) = (209.557^\circ, 26.553^\circ) \pm 0.3^\circ$ derived from the iterative-median of the GMM members (Section~\ref{section:membership}), offset by $\sim 20'$ ($\sim 0.6\,r_h$) south-east of the \citet{Grillmair2009} discovery position. From five \gaia\ DR3 RR~Lyrae stars calibrated via \citet{Garofalo2022} we obtain a new heliocentric distance $d_\odot = 48.5 \pm 1.9$~kpc (Section~\ref{section:distance}). Combined with the \citet{Moskowitz2020} angular size, this yields a Wolf-half-light dynamical mass $M_{1/2} \approx 1.24 \times 10^{6}\,M_\odot$.

\item \textbf{Orbit and tidal stream modeling.} Orbit integrations confirm that Boo~III follows a highly eccentric ($e \approx 0.82$ with LMC), retrograde, polar orbit with a very recent pericentric passage ($\sim 0.13$~Gyr ago at $r_{\mathrm{peri}} \approx 9.5$~kpc). Using \texttt{galpy}'s particle spray code, we simulate tidal streams and show that the predicted stream track and kinematics are sensitive to the MW halo mass, LMC mass, and Solar velocity. The simulated streams are broadly consistent with the Styx stream, supporting a physical association between the two systems.

\item \textbf{Evidence for active tidal disruption.} Three independent diagnostics consistently favour active tidal stripping. (i) The combination of a small pericentre ($r_{\rm peri} \approx 9.5$~kpc) and a very recent pericentric passage ($\sim 0.13$~Gyr ago) means Boo~III has just emerged from the strongest instantaneous tidal field of its orbit and has not had time to relax back to dynamical equilibrium. (ii) Evaluated at pericentre, the King tidal radius is $r_t \approx 164$~pc $\approx 0.3\,r_h$, i.e.\ substantially smaller than the system's half-light radius, so material outside the inner $\sim 0.3\,r_h$ is dynamically unbound at closest approach. (iii) Equivalently, Boo~III's mean half-light density is more than an order of magnitude below twice the mean enclosed Milky-Way density at its orbital pericentre --- well below the threshold that \citet{Pace2022} identify as marking the onset of tidal disruption. All three diagnostics agree and place Boo~III squarely in the ``actively disrupting'' regime, alongside Antlia~2, Crater~2, Sagittarius, and Tucana~III. Boo~III is, by a clear margin, one of the faintest and least massive satellites with such direct disruption signatures.

\item \textbf{Comparison with the Typhon stream.} Boo~III and the Typhon stream occupy similar regions in action and integrals-of-motion space, consistent with their shared polar orbit and high eccentricity. However, the two systems have distinct metallicities, ruling out a direct progenitor--debris relationship. Instead, their orbital similarity suggests they may share a common infall origin.

\item \textbf{Tidal-tail candidates and a path to potential constraints.} Extending the simple box selection of Section~\ref{section:membership} (Eqs.~\ref{eq:boxcut_rv}--\ref{eq:boxcut_feh}) beyond the 3 $r_h$ ellipse yields six \SSSSS\ DR2 candidates: two RGB stars at $v_{\rm hel} \approx 190$~\vlosUnits\ with $[\mathrm{Fe/H}] \approx -2.6$ that are kinematic and chemical analogues of the inner GMM-selected members, and four likely halo contaminants clustered at $v_{\rm hel} \approx 170$~\vlosUnits\ (Appendix~\ref{section:appendix_tail}). These two likely tail members are overplotted in green stars in the $\phi_1$ stream diagnostic of Figure~\ref{fig:phi1_stream}; both sit close to the predicted spray track in radial velocity, with a slight preference for the \texttt{McMillan17} or a lighter MW potential rather than the heavier scaled-up alternatives. This is consistent with the broader observation that the orbit- and stream-based velocity-gradient diagnostic of Section~\ref{section:gradient} is sensitive to the MW halo mass, the LMC mass, the solar reflex, and Boo~III's own 6D phase-space coordinates --- so a future confirmation of the Boo~III tail (with even a modest number of spectroscopic members at $|\phi_1| \gtrsim 0.5^\circ$) would constrain the joint MW + LMC potential through the gradient predictions, with the dominant 6D-side uncertainty coming from the heliocentric distance (Appendix~\ref{section:appendix_6dmc}). Our broader photometric and astrometric searches using BHB and RR~Lyrae stars did not yield an extended detection of the predicted tail itself, the primary obstacle being contamination from the Sagittarius stream, which overlaps with the predicted Boo~III tail in sky position, proper motion, and distance, but differs by $\gtrsim 100$~\vlosUnits\ in radial velocity. Deep spectroscopic follow-up along the predicted stream track therefore offers the most promising path both to confirming the Boo~III--Styx connection and to using the recovered tail members as a potential probe.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Tidal-tail candidates beyond 3 $r_h$} \label{section:appendix_tail}


The simple box selection of Eqs.~\ref{eq:boxcut_rv}--\ref{eq:boxcut_feh} provides an opportunity to extend the Boo~III member search beyond the 3 $r_h$ ellipse used for the systemic-property fit in Section~\ref{section:membership}.
Applied to the parent \SSSSS\ DR2 catalog with the same quality-control (QC) cuts, \gaia\ DR3 RR~Lyrae crossmatches removed, and the spatial cut now \emph{relaxed} to allow stars beyond the 3 $r_h$ ellipse, the box returns six candidates between $3.1\,r_h$ and $5.8\,r_h$ from the centre (i.e.\ between $1.0\times$ and $1.9\times$ the 3 $r_h$ ellipse semi-major axis); the exact $r/r_h$ values are listed in the final column of Table~\ref{tab:appendix_candidates}.
Their full S5 DR2 properties are listed in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:appendix_candidates}; the four-panel diagnostic plot of Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_candidates} highlights them in the same observables as Figure~\ref{fig:main_members}.

The six candidates fall into two cleanly separated subgroups in radial velocity.
\emph{Two stars} (\gaia\ DR3 1451743686723428352 and 1445105484053956352) are kinematic and chemical analogues of the 21 GMM members: $v_{\rm hel} = 186.8$ and $193.1$~\vlosUnits, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}] = -2.69$ and $-2.59$~dex, with $\log g \approx 3.2$ and $3.5$ consistent with red-giant-branch (RGB) stars at the distance of Boo~III.
Their locations at $r = 3.7\,r_h$ and $5.8\,r_h$ from the centre ($1.2\times$ and $1.9\times$ the 3 $r_h$ ellipse semi-major axis), combined with proper motions and CMD positions consistent with the bound population, make them strong candidates for tidal-tail members.
The remaining \emph{four stars} cluster tightly at $v_{\rm hel} \approx 170$--$175$~\vlosUnits, well below the systemic 191~\vlosUnits, and are most plausibly explained as a chance halo subpopulation. Two strong, independent pieces of evidence support this interpretation. First, their metallicities $[\mathrm{Fe/H}] = -1.31$, $-1.47$, $-1.94$, and $-2.02$ (Table~\ref{tab:appendix_candidates}) lie systematically above Boo~III's mean of $\overline{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]} = -2.34 \pm 0.11$~dex --- by between $0.3$ and $1.0$~dex --- so they are chemically discrepant with the bound population by more than its intrinsic metallicity dispersion ($\sigma_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]} = 0.36$~dex; Section~\ref{section:membership}). Second, their kinematics are inconsistent with the GMM-fit systemic by $\gtrsim 5\sigma$ given $\sigma_v = 1.7$~\vlosUnits, and one of them (\gaia\ DR3 1251933905373373184) has $\log g = 4.1$, essentially certifying it as a foreground main-sequence dwarf.

We consider the two RGB-like, low-[Fe/H] candidates at $v_{\rm hel} \approx 190$~\vlosUnits\ to be plausible tidal-tail members but do not formally include them in the systemic-property fit of Section~\ref{section:membership}: the 3 $r_h$ ellipse there was chosen as a sensible compromise --- it is large enough to enclose the bulk of the centrally-concentrated GMM members but small enough that the MW field background does not overwhelm the dwarf component, ensuring that the GMM converges on the dwarf parameters cleanly. Extending the spatial cut further introduces enough additional MW contamination to bias the fit and, in extreme cases, prevents convergence on the dwarf component altogether.
Their existence motivates the deeper-survey strategy outlined in Section~\ref{section:tail_prospects}:
targeted spectroscopy outside the 3 $r_h$ ellipse along the predicted stream track from Section~\ref{section:orbit}, with $v_{\rm hel}$ as the primary discriminant against halo and Sagittarius-stream contamination.

\section{6D Phase-Space Uncertainty Study on the Orbit} \label{section:appendix_6dmc}

To assess how each component of Boo~III's 6D phase-space uncertainty propagates into the inferred orbit, we run a per-component Monte Carlo using the \texttt{McMillan17}~+~LMC potential (the same fiducial as Section~\ref{section:orbit}). For each of the six 6D parameters --- $(\alpha, \delta, D_\odot, \mu_\alpha\cos\delta, \mu_\delta, v_{\rm hel})$ --- we draw 100 perturbed values from a Gaussian centred on our fiducial measurement and with width equal to the corresponding $1\sigma$ uncertainty from Table~\ref{tab:params} ($\pm 0.3^\circ$ for the centroid, $\pm 1.9$~kpc for the distance, and the GMM posterior $1\sigma$ for the systemic velocity and proper motion), holding the other five parameters fixed at their fiducial values. We integrate each perturbed orbit forward and backward by $\pm 0.5$~Gyr in the \texttt{McMillan17}~+~LMC potential and record the orbital trajectory in the spectroscopic and astrometric observables.

\begin{figure*}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figA4_orbit_per_component.pdf}
    \caption{Per-6D-component orbit Monte Carlo for Boo~III in the McMillan17 + LMC potential. Each column varies a single 6D parameter (held at fiducial $\pm$ Table~\ref{tab:params} $1\sigma$, colour-coded by the perturbed value via viridis); each row is an orbital observable. The 100 perturbed-orbit trajectories per panel sit around the fiducial (black) and the present-day measurement (blue ``P'' marker). The distance row produces by far the widest spread in every observable, identifying $D_\odot$ as the dominant orbit-uncertainty driver --- consistent with the gradient breakdown in Section~\ref{section:gradient}.}

Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_per_component} shows the resulting six-by-five panel grid: each row varies a single 6D parameter, and each column shows one orbital observable as a function of right ascension along the orbit. The fiducial trajectory is overplotted as a black curve in each panel, and the present-day position is marked with a red star. The 100 perturbed orbits per panel are drawn as faint blue lines; the visual width of the bundle is a direct proxy for the contribution of that component to the orbit uncertainty. The distance row dominates: a $\pm 1.9$~kpc spread in $D_\odot$ produces the broadest bundle in every observable, especially in the radial-velocity and distance-modulus rows where the trajectory is most sensitive to the satellite's instantaneous Galactocentric position. The proper-motion components contribute a smaller but visible spread, while the centroid (RA, Dec) and systemic velocity contribute negligible spread on this scale.

We can quantify the same per-component contribution at the level of the predicted Boo~III line-of-sight velocity gradient components $\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\xi$ and $\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\eta$ used in Section~\ref{section:gradient}. For each 100-realisation per-component sub-MC, we evaluate the gradient by linearising the orbit's $v_{\rm los}(\phi)$ track at $\pm 1^\circ$ arc-length around the present-day position and project onto $(\hat\xi, \hat\eta)$. Table~\ref{tab:grad_budget} reports the resulting $1\sigma$ spreads. The total budget is dominated by $D_\odot$, which alone contributes $\sigma[\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\xi] \approx 1.47$ and $\sigma[\partial v_{\rm los}/\partial\eta] \approx 0.20$~\vlosUnits~deg$^{-1}$; this is consistent with the full-6D error bar shown on the $f=1$ orbit-prediction diamond in Figure~\ref{fig:fig7_gradient} (top-right panel). The proper-motion components contribute $\sim 5\times$ less, and the centroid + systemic-velocity contributions are negligible at this level. The central message is that the dominant uncertainty in the predicted gradient comes from the heliocentric distance, not from the centroid or PM precision --- so for any future improvement on the gradient prediction, a better RRL-based distance is the highest-leverage quantity to target.
